Dear Reader,

Hi. I'm Edvard.

I look at very old stories—from before your grandparents' grandparents were born. I wonder about something: did someone plan the whole world from the start, like a recipe? I don't think so. I think things just grow, step by step, all on their own. Like a tiny seed. Nobody tells the seed exactly how to become a tree. It just grows, a little bit each day, and becomes something big. That's what I study. How things grow and change without a plan.

As I study ancient history and these old ideas about time, the questions get bigger: why do we exist, and where is the world going? The future isn't written out ahead of time like an instruction manual. Instead, people and cultures slowly change and create new meanings over time—the way a caterpillar doesn't follow a blueprint to become a butterfly. It just transforms, one stage at a time, into something nobody could have predicted just by looking at the caterpillar alone. I want to understand how that happens with ideas and societies, not just animals.

To name it properly, I study religion and ancient philosophy—specifically, how ancient cultures handled big questions about time and purpose. My central idea is that human purpose isn't something handed down from above by a design or a creator. It develops. Societies build new ideas on top of old ones, generation after generation, and meaning grows out of that process rather than being installed at the start. To see how this works, I am drawn to texts that describe big, hard-to-name things—like God, or time itself—by saying what they are not, rather than trying to pin down what they are. This habit of thought, negation instead of definition, is one of the oldest tools humans have for approaching concepts too massive to fully describe.

To be precise about my position, I am an undergraduate student and amateur unpublished researcher in religious studies at Uppsala University, born in 1989. I am building a parallel reading track alongside my coursework—self-directed, still early, and mostly through secondary sources and closed-forum discussion rather than sustained primary-text work so far. My explicit work investigates the structural intersection of Gāthic philosophy—the earliest Zoroastrian texts, whose core frameworks I track via resources like The Zarathushtrian Assembly—and the apophatic theology of time: characterizing time or the divine through exclusion rather than positive definition. I utilize mathematical structuralism and Indo-Iranian nondualism as auxiliary frameworks to contextualize these process-driven timelines, drawing on resources such as Indo-Iranian nondual tantra.

Methodologically, I work from monism and the dialectical method—Spinoza to Hegel—treating experience as a fragment internal to that method, staged across the movements of consciousness and self-consciousness. Consequently, I trace interpretation as Gadamer’s pointed break from this lineage, occurring at the exact junction where Hegel sought historical closure in Absolute Knowing and Gadamer refused it, opting instead for an open, non-terminating exposure to the text. I deploy these phenomenological, hermeneutic, and discourse-analytic methodologies to interrogate the architecture of these deep wisdom traditions.

The claim I hold with the most confidence, independent of that lineage, is a strictly naturalistic, non-teleological account of telos: ultimate purpose is neither structural design nor theological decree, but an emergent property. It is generated naturalistically as complex cultural systems traverse the adjacent possible toward stable attractor states. The live question is not merely how this dynamic has played out historically over the last 4,000 years across the deep histories of the Gāthās and the tantras, but what is reachable right now, from where we stand, given everything that has already been built. The core of my work is the present-tense mapping of this "adjacent possible"; the historical texts and the philosophical lineage are the scaffolding I am constructing underneath it to check my thinking against something older than my own assumptions.

Sincerely,
Pontus Edvard Samani
