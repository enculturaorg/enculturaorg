We have compassion towards those with the most energy among us, and we respect the wisdom of those more competent than us.

Pontus Edvard Samani is an undergraduate student and amateur unpublished researcher in religious studies, focused on ancient Zoroastrian philosophy (found in texts called the Gāthās) and on how certain traditions describe time and the divine using negation rather than direct description (called "apophatic theology"). He also studies mathematical structuralism and nondual traditions from the Indo-Iranian world, using tools like phenomenology (the study of experience) and hermeneutics (the study of interpretation). His central argument: purpose isn't designed by a creator or fixed in advance. It emerges naturally as cultures evolve, exploring new possibilities one step at a time — a process he sees recorded across 2,000 years in both the Gāthās and the tantras.

Niches of Conceptual Emergence

My research operates at the intersection of diverse structural lineages. Having grown up within an exiled Iranian and Protestant Christian background—traditions centered primarily around discrete historical "Events"—it has taken me many years to reconnect with ancient Eastern wisdom and grasp its process-oriented component, which is inherently non-dogmatic.
